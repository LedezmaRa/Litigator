Executive Summary: The Economic Machine Right Now

Let me start from first principles and explain what the data is telling us about where we are in the economic cycle.

The dominant narrative is one of stagflationary pressure meeting a late-cycle economy. Here's why I say that:

When I look across all 11 sectors, three macro signals scream at me: (1) crude oil up 71% in 90 days, (2) 10-Year Treasury yields grinding higher at 4.22%, and (3) industrial metals and cyclical proxies rolling over hard (copper miners down 12% in a month, regional banks down 12%, homebuilders down 17%). This combination is the textbook setup for margin compression and growth deceleration.

Let me explain the mechanism. Oil is an input cost for virtually every business in America. When crude surges 71% in three months, it acts as a tax on the economy—consumers pay more at the pump, airlines pay more for jet fuel, trucking companies pay more for diesel, and manufacturers pay more for petrochemical feedstocks. This is inflationary in the short term. But here's the crucial second-order effect: the Federal Reserve sees this inflation and keeps rates elevated (hence the 10Y at 4.22%, trending higher). Higher rates then crush rate-sensitive sectors—housing, autos, anything financed. So you get inflation AND slowing growth simultaneously. That's stagflation.

The credit and cyclical signals confirm this. Regional banks (KRE down 12% in a month) are the canary in the coal mine for credit conditions. Banks are struggling because (a) their bond portfolios are underwater from rate hikes, (b) commercial real estate loans are souring, and (c) net interest margins are compressing as deposit costs rise faster than loan yields. When regional banks weaken, credit availability tightens, and that's the transmission mechanism through which monetary policy actually slows the economy.

Meanwhile, the yield curve structure tells us something important. The 3-Month T-Bill at 3.61% versus the 10-Year at 4.22% gives us a modestly positive slope of ~60 basis points. This is notable because we've been inverted for much of the past two years. A steepening curve after inversion historically precedes recessions by 6-12 months—the economy is slowing, but the Fed hasn't cut yet. We're in that window.

The risk appetite picture is bifurcated. Bitcoin—my favorite measure of speculative froth—is down 25% over 90 days and sits 43% off its highs. Semiconductors, however, are up 13% over 90 days. This divergence tells me we're not in a broad risk-on environment; rather, there's a narrow AI-driven bid in tech that's masking underlying weakness. When Bitcoin and semis diverge this sharply, it usually means the speculative tide is going out, but a few boats are still floating on fundamental stories.

Bottom line: We are in late-cycle, facing an energy-driven inflation impulse that will keep the Fed on hold (or hawkish), while credit conditions tighten and cyclical demand rolls over. This is an environment that rewards selectivity and defensiveness, not broad beta exposure.
